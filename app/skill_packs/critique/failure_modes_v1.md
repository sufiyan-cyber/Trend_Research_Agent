# Skill Pack: Campaign Failure Modes (v1)

The vocabulary for the negative section. A critique that names a mode from this table is specific; a critique that says "engagement may vary" is noise.

## Strategy-level failures

| Mode | Signature | How to spot it in the material |
|---|---|---|
| **Attention without transfer** | People remember the joke, not the brand | Brand appears only as a logo bug; the creative idea is separable from the product; the punchline would work for any advertiser |
| **Wrong-audience virality** | Reach came from people who will never buy | Humour/format aimed at a broad internet audience while the product serves a narrow buyer; comment section is peers and marketers, not customers |
| **Borrowed relevance** | The moment carries it, not the idea | Remove the event/meme and nothing remains; a dozen brands ran a near-identical post the same day |
| **Play/funnel mismatch** | Top-of-funnel creative with a bottom-of-funnel ask | Hard purchase CTA on a cold-audience awareness piece, or no CTA on a conversion push |
| **Undifferentiated in-category** | Competitors could ship it unchanged | Category-convention visuals, generic claim, nothing only this brand could say |
| **One-shot format** | Cannot become a series | The idea depends on a single coincidence; no repeatable template |
| **Sophistication mismatch** | Talks past or down to the buyer | Jargon for beginners, or over-explaining to experts |

## Execution-level failures

| Mode | Signature | How to spot it |
|---|---|---|
| **Buried hook** | The good line is not the first line | Strongest phrase sits mid-copy or below the fold/fold-line of the crop |
| **Text-density overload** | Unreadable at feed size | Body copy that fails at thumbnail scale; more than ~3 competing focal points |
| **Contrast/accessibility failure** | A share of the audience literally cannot read it | Low-contrast type over imagery, meaning carried by colour alone, no captions on a talking-head video, text baked into an image with no alt path |
| **Unsafe crop** | Platform UI eats the message | Key content in the caption-safe/UI zone; 9:16 content posted where it renders 4:5 |
| **Claim without receipt** | Asserts a number nobody can check | "10,000+ placed" with no source, screenshot, or named example |
| **CTA friction** | The ask costs more than the motivation | DM-for-link, off-platform jump, form before value, unclear next step |

## Reasoning failures — the ones that bite the *analysis*, not the campaign

These apply to the report you are reviewing, and belong in `what_we_could_not_verify` or `why_it_might_not_work`.

- **Survivorship** — you are looking at a campaign because it surfaced. The identical play that flopped was never posted, screenshotted, or sent to this pipeline.
- **Post-hoc attribution** — the campaign ran and the metric moved, so the campaign moved it. Seasonality, a concurrent promotion, a price change, paid spend behind the organic post, or an unrelated news cycle are all live alternatives.
- **Confusing spend with craft** — heavy paid distribution makes mediocre creative look like a hit. Reach is buyable; a screenshot does not show the media budget.
- **Reading intent from output** — a polished result does not prove a deliberate strategy. Some of what reads as clever targeting is a house style, an agency template, or an accident.
- **Engagement ≠ outcome** — comments, saves and shares are not revenue, and comment volume is often *disagreement*. Ratio matters and is usually invisible in a crop.
- **Single-frame inference** — one screenshot of a multi-asset campaign shows one asset. The sequence, the retargeting, and the landing page are all missing.
- **Our-brand projection** — "it worked for them" carries an unstated assumption that their audience, follower base, and brand permission resemble ours.

## Copying risk — for `risk_if_we_copy_it`

Ask each in turn and report the ones that actually bite:

1. **Audience** — does our bucket share the pain this hooks? Our audiences are students and career-switchers, not the general consumer internet.
2. **Permission** — does our brand have standing to say this? Contrarian and controversy plays need earned authority; a challenger brand's swagger reads as arrogance from a newcomer.
3. **Production reality** — can we actually make it? Billboards, celebrity talent, film crews and location shoots are not neutral asks.
4. **Channel** — the play may be native to a platform where our audience is not, or where we have no distribution.
5. **Timing** — a `peaked` or `fading` verdict means we would arrive as the tenth brand doing it, which reads as derivative rather than topical.
6. **Fatigue** — have we already run this play recently? Repetition on the same list burns it faster than the market does.
